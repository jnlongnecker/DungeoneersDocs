## Philosophy

Dungeoneers is meant to be playable as not just a TTRPG, but also as a video game. I play 5e 2014 with my friends from out of state, and ran into countless creative blockers using existing online tabletop tools. I also want this game to be playable solo with an algorithm (read: not AI) acting as GM. While this aspect doesn't have anything to do with the attached rules, it is a pervasive requirement that permeates all hard rules: it _must_ translate to a video game scenario.

The goal of the game is to produce a heroic, high fantasy experience that allows for players to create and play the character within their mind's eye without constantly fighting the rule system to allow that character to exist. The player characters (dungeoneers) are powerful, competent explorers that deal with threats far greater than the common man is equipped to deal with. This goal has major effects on many core parts of the game.

## Classless

Classes are useful for their simplicity and standardization, but are often too rigid to fully encapsulate all manner of character ideas. Often times, one will squeeze a character into a class or set of classes that don't entirely fit or sometimes not even make narrative sense. Removing classes allows for a power system more piecemeal and more flexible to fit character builds more closely.

## Combat Options

All characters should have several options available to them in all circumstances, but primarily so in combat. Martial type characters should have more capabilities than just attacking in the same/similar way each time. Level advancement should not only make characters more powerful, but also give them more tools. These tools should not be rigid, they should be flexible. Players should be able to try things out and not be punished for "picking wrong" in character creation.

## Magic

Magic is meant to be mysterious and an area of active research within the world. There are 3 kinds of "magical" abilities: Blessings, Invocations, and Enchantments. Blessings are granted from worship, and are magical rewards unlocked from devotion. Invocations are modifiers on existing abilities; you use these to increase power, range, apply effects, sculpt areas, and (in general) bend an ability to your whim in the moment. Enchantments are pre-programmed magical spells that are powerful and not yet understood. They are locked into items that people must use in order to activate.

## Faith

In many systems, faith is merely flavoring to a character. In Dungeoneers, your faith matters. In order to be eligible for the boons of religion, you must actually show your devotion through worship rituals. The flexible and "lossless" idealogy of Dungeoneers applies here to ensure that losing favor with one power does not result in you missing sufficient favor to unlock as many Blessings as you are eligible for.

## Equipment that Matters

What equipment you have with you is important, as each item has a purpose and grants some kind of ability. Space for loot is limited, as it conflicts with your space for useful dungeoneering tools. Planning ahead and doing research on a dungeon will allow for better planning of resources. Is the area rich in food? Rations may take up too much space. Does the dungeon have many cave-ins? Better bring a shovel & pickaxe (unless you've got a party member with burrowing claws).

## Rules as the Baseline

The rules are there to give concrete and expansive options for players to use and for GMs to simply read and interpret. Due to the desire to have Dungeoneers double as a video game, these options need to capture a satisfactory degree of freedom. Should a player wish to do something creative, the rules must either have a way to do that action by some combination of powers or should give the GM enough of a baseline to understand how to arbitrate that creative action. Some actions exist solely to aid in this kind of creative exploration by giving examples of extremely flexible actions and how to arbitrate them.
